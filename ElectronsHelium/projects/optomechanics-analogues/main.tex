\begin{abstract}
An electron trapped above superfluid helium and dipole-coupled to a superconducting microwave
resonator is, formally, a cavity-optomechanical system: a bosonic cavity mode coupled to a bosonic
mechanical (here, in-plane motional) mode. Landmark protocols developed for cavity optomechanics —
resolved-sideband cooling of a mechanical mode to its ground
state~\cite{teufel2011sideband}, single-mode motional squeezing via two-tone
driving~\cite{pirkkalainen2015squeezing}, and dissipative (reservoir-engineered) entanglement between
two mechanical oscillators via a shared cavity mode~\cite{ockeloenkorppi2018stabilized} — have not,
to our knowledge, been translated to the electrons-on-helium (eHe) platform. The prerequisite for all
three, a single electron strongly coupled to a microwave photon ($g \gg \kappa,\Gamma_2$), has now
been experimentally demonstrated on exactly this platform, with a measured coupling rate
$g/2\pi = 118\pm3\,\mathrm{MHz}$, resonator linewidth $\kappa/2\pi = 23\,\mathrm{MHz}$, and electron
decoherence rate as low as $\Gamma_2/2\pi = 61\,\mathrm{MHz}$, corresponding to a single-photon
cooperativity $\mathcal{C} = 4g^2/(\kappa\Gamma_2) \approx 32$, in a dilution-refrigerator device
operated down to 7~mK~\cite{koolstra2026strong}. Building on demonstrated high-impedance resonator
coupling~\cite{koolstra2025impedance}, demonstrated dispersive charge/motional
readout~\cite{castoria2025}, and now this strong-coupling milestone~\cite{koolstra2026strong}, we
outline how each of the three optomechanics protocols maps onto the eHe Hamiltonian, evaluate the
resolved-sideband and cooperativity budget against these newly measured device parameters, and
discuss how the resulting entanglement generation compares with the existing, purely coherent,
direct-Coulomb-coupling route already used on this platform~\cite{beysengulov2024}. This is a
proposal manuscript: the strong-coupling and resolved-sideband prerequisites are now experimentally
established, but no cooling, squeezing, or entangling protocol has itself yet been demonstrated or
simulated in detail against these parameters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Cavity optomechanics couples a mechanical oscillator's position to an optical or microwave cavity
field through radiation pressure, giving access to a standard toolkit of linearized interactions
(beam-splitter and two-mode-squeezing terms, selected by the drive detuning) that has produced, among
other results, ground-state cooling of a micromechanical
resonator~\cite{teufel2011sideband}, squeezing of a mechanical mode below the standard quantum
limit~\cite{pirkkalainen2015squeezing}, and — the result most directly relevant to this
platform's existing entanglement work — dissipatively stabilized entanglement between two
\emph{massive, spatially separated} mechanical oscillators via their shared coupling to a common
cavity mode~\cite{ockeloenkorppi2018stabilized}. A comprehensive review of the field is given in
Ref.~\cite{aspelmeyer2014cavity}.

The electrons-on-helium platform has, formally, the same ingredients: an in-plane electron motional
mode plays the role of the mechanical oscillator, and a superconducting microwave resonator plays
the role of the optical cavity, with the electric-dipole coupling $g$ playing the role of the
optomechanical coupling. This coupling was first engineered toward the strong-coupling regime through
high-impedance resonator design~\cite{koolstra2025impedance}, and dispersive readout of electron
motion was demonstrated experimentally, including operation above 1~K~\cite{castoria2025}. The
strong-coupling regime itself has now been reached: Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong} reports a single
electron on helium coupled to a high-kinetic-inductance TiN resonator with measured coupling rate
$g/2\pi = 118\pm3\,\mathrm{MHz}$ (in agreement with the $g/2\pi\approx110\,\mathrm{MHz}$ design value
of Ref.~\cite{koolstra2025impedance}), resonator linewidth $\kappa/2\pi = 23\,\mathrm{MHz}$, and
electron decoherence rate $\Gamma_2/2\pi = 75\pm5\,\mathrm{MHz}$ (improving to
$\Gamma_2/2\pi = 61\,\mathrm{MHz}$ with negative electrode voltage offsets), unambiguously confirmed
via vacuum Rabi splitting in the resonator transmission spectrum. This is, to our knowledge, the
first demonstration that this platform actually sits deep in the strong-coupling, resolved-sideband
regime this project's protocols require — see Sec.~\ref{sec:cooling} for the explicit budget. What
has \emph{not} yet been done — on this platform, to our knowledge — is to drive the
resonator--electron system at the red or blue mechanical sideband to access the cooling, squeezing,
and entangling protocols listed above, as opposed to the vacuum-Rabi-splitting spectroscopy and
dispersive (weak-coupling, static-shift) readout used so far. Notably, Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong}
itself anticipates this direction in its own outlook, noting that its strong-coupling result "opens
access to the investigation of a range of new light–matter phenomena with a single fundamental
particle" and to "exotic ultrastrong coupling regimes of circuit quantum optics" — a broader framing
into which the three specific protocols pursued here (cooling, squeezing, entanglement) fit as
concrete, well-established special cases.

There is also a direct, and useful, methodological connection already present in this project: the
existing entanglement work on this platform~\cite{beysengulov2024} generates motional entanglement
between two electrons through their \emph{direct, coherent} Coulomb interaction — no dissipation
involved. The optomechanical entanglement protocol of Ref.~\cite{ockeloenkorppi2018stabilized}, in
contrast, is \emph{dissipative}: entanglement is stabilized (not merely generated transiently) by
engineering the coupling of two oscillators to a shared, damped cavity mode, such that the steady
state of the driven-dissipative dynamics is itself entangled. Comparing these two entangling
mechanisms — coherent Coulomb coupling vs.\ dissipative reservoir engineering — on the same physical
platform, with the same device parameters, is a genuinely new comparison this project can make.
(We also note, as a matter of record rather than a scientific argument, that F.~Massel — a co-author
of the eHe sensing proposal~\cite{perruzza2026} — is also a co-author of both the squeezing and
entanglement optomechanics results cited above~\cite{pirkkalainen2015squeezing,ockeloenkorppi2018stabilized},
so the relevant expertise already overlaps with this collaboration.)

\section{Model}

\subsection{Linearized optomechanical Hamiltonian for eHe}

Following the standard optomechanics linearization (see Ref.~\cite{aspelmeyer2014cavity}), write the
resonator (annihilation operator $a$, frequency $\omega_r$) coupled to the electron's in-plane
motional mode (annihilation operator $b$, frequency $\omega_e$) as
\begin{equation}
H_0 = \hbar\omega_r a^\dagger a + \hbar \omega_e b^\dagger b + \hbar g\,(a + a^\dagger)(b + b^\dagger),
\end{equation}
with $g$ the electron--photon coupling of Refs.~\cite{koolstra2025impedance,castoria2025,koolstra2026strong}
(there, $g = \tfrac{1}{2} e E_x \omega_0 \sqrt{Z_{\rm res}/(m_e \omega_e)}$; measured/simulated values
range from $g/2\pi = 9.8\,\mathrm{MHz}$ (dispersive charge-readout device~\cite{castoria2025}) up to
the now-\emph{measured} strong-coupling value $g/2\pi = 118\pm3\,\mathrm{MHz}$
($Z_{\rm res} = 3.8\,\mathrm{k}\Omega$, a $\sim20\times$ boost over earlier
realizations)~\cite{koolstra2026strong} — see \texttt{resonator-coupling} and \texttt{charge-readout}).
Driving the resonator at a
detuning $\Delta = \omega_d - \omega_r$ and linearizing around the resulting large coherent
resonator amplitude gives, after the standard rotating-wave approximation valid when
$g \ll \omega_e, \kappa \ll \omega_e$ (resolved-sideband regime):
\begin{align}
\Delta = +\omega_e &: \quad H \propto g\,(a^\dagger b + a b^\dagger) \quad \text{(beam-splitter; cooling/state-swap)}, \\
\Delta = -\omega_e &: \quad H \propto g\,(a^\dagger b^\dagger + a b) \quad \text{(two-mode squeezing; heating/entangling)}.
\end{align}
The red-sideband (beam-splitter) interaction is the basis of sideband cooling
(Sec.~\ref{sec:cooling}); the blue-sideband (two-mode-squeezing) interaction is the basis of
motional squeezing (Sec.~\ref{sec:squeezing}) and, extended to two electrons each coupled to a
shared or coupled-resonator mode, of dissipative entanglement (Sec.~\ref{sec:entanglement}).

\subsection{Sideband cooling (analogue of Ref.~\cite{teufel2011sideband})}
\label{sec:cooling}

Red-sideband driving realizes a beam-splitter interaction that swaps excitations from the electron
motional mode into the (lossy) resonator mode, cooling the electron's in-plane motion toward its
ground state at an effective rate set by the cooperativity $C = 4g^2/(\kappa\Gamma)$. This budget can
now be evaluated directly against measured numbers rather than design projections:
Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong} reports $g/2\pi = 118\pm3\,\mathrm{MHz}$, a (loaded) resonator
linewidth $\kappa/2\pi = 23\,\mathrm{MHz}$, and an electron decoherence rate as low as
$\Gamma_2/2\pi = 61\,\mathrm{MHz}$, giving a single-photon cooperativity
$\mathcal{C} = 4g^2/(\kappa\Gamma_2) \approx 32 \gg 1$. The electron motional frequency is tunable
over $\omega_e/2\pi \approx 5.5$--$8.7\,\mathrm{GHz}$, so $\kappa/\omega_e \sim 3\times10^{-3} \ll 1$:
the device is deep in the resolved-sideband regime. \textbf{This resolves the open question from the
previous version of this note}: the demonstrated device \emph{does} sit in the resolved-sideband,
high-cooperativity regime nominally required for efficient sideband cooling, without needing the
further impedance increases once projected in Ref.~\cite{koolstra2025impedance}
($g/2\pi\approx80\,\mathrm{MHz}$) — the realized coupling already exceeds that projection.
\textbf{Remaining open question:} $\Gamma_2$ in Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong} is dominated by pure
dephasing rather than energy relaxation ($\gamma_1 \ll \Gamma_2$, with $T_1 \approx 0.76\,\mu$s at
large detuning), and $\Gamma_2$ itself grows with temperature as a $T^{0.5}$ power law from 7~mK to
450~mK with no sign of saturating at the lowest measured temperature. Standard sideband-cooling
formulas (final phonon occupation $n_f \approx (\kappa/4\omega_e)^2$ in the ideal resolved-sideband
limit, modified by $\Gamma_2/g$) assume a well-characterized, temperature-independent bath; whether
the observed dephasing-dominated, apparently non-saturating noise source (tentatively attributed to
ripplons or stray-charge fluctuations~\cite{koolstra2026strong}) limits the achievable cooling floor
in a way the standard formula does not capture is the calculation this project still needs to do.

\subsection{Motional squeezing (analogue of Ref.~\cite{pirkkalainen2015squeezing})}
\label{sec:squeezing}

Two-tone driving (simultaneous red and blue sidebands with unequal amplitude) squeezes one
quadrature of the electron's motional state below the zero-point level, at the cost of anti-squeezing
the conjugate quadrature, exactly as in Ref.~\cite{pirkkalainen2015squeezing}. The operating-point
question flagged in the previous version of this note is now partly settled:
Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong} operates and characterizes the strongly coupled device across
$7\,\mathrm{mK}$--$450\,\mathrm{mK}$ in a dilution refrigerator, not the $1.1\,\mathrm{K}$ regime of
the dispersive charge-readout device~\cite{castoria2025} — for a $\sim$7--9~GHz mode, $7\,\mathrm{mK}$
gives a thermal occupation $\bar n = 1/(e^{\hbar\omega_e/k_BT}-1) \ll 1$, i.e.\ the device is
operated close to its motional ground state \emph{before} any active sideband cooling is applied,
unlike the >1~K charge-sensing regime. \textbf{Open question:} the achievable squeezing level in dB
is still unevaluated — it requires combining the now-known $g$, $\kappa$, and $\Gamma_2(T)$ with the
standard two-tone-squeezing formula (see \texttt{aspelmeyer2014cavity}) and checking whether the
observed non-saturating, dephasing-dominated $\Gamma_2$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:cooling}) imposes a squeezing
ceiling below the level $\mathcal{C}\approx32$ would naively suggest.

\subsection{Motional entanglement (analogue of Ref.~\cite{ockeloenkorppi2018stabilized})}
\label{sec:entanglement}

Two electrons, each dipole-coupled to a shared resonator mode (or to two resonator modes coupled to
each other), driven on their respective blue sidebands, can have their motional degrees of freedom
driven into a steady-state entangled state via the same reservoir-engineering mechanism as
Ref.~\cite{ockeloenkorppi2018stabilized} — dissipation through the (lossy) shared cavity mode
\emph{stabilizes} rather than destroys the entanglement, provided the engineered dissipative
channel dominates the individual decoherence channels of each electron. This is conceptually
distinct from the coherent, Coulomb-driven entanglement mechanism already used on this platform
(configurations II/III of Ref.~\cite{beysengulov2024}): that mechanism requires the two electrons to
be close enough to interact directly and entangles motional \emph{and}, via the singlet/triplet
construction, spin degrees of freedom; the dissipative mechanism proposed here entangles motional
degrees of freedom of two electrons that need not be Coulomb-coupled at all, only both coupled to a
shared cavity mode — potentially a route to entangling electrons that are too far apart to interact
directly via Coulomb repulsion. Unlike the cooling and squeezing protocols above, this channel is
\emph{not} directly addressed by Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong}, which demonstrates strong coupling
for a single electron in a single dot; the measured $g$, $\kappa$, and $\Gamma_2$ are nonetheless the
right per-electron numbers to plug into a two-electron, shared- (or coupled-) resonator extension of
that device once designed. \textbf{Open question, now sharpened:} given a per-electron cooperativity
$\mathcal{C}\approx32$ is achievable (Sec.~\ref{sec:cooling}), what steady-state logarithmic
negativity does the driven-dissipative two-oscillator theory of
Ref.~\cite{ockeloenkorppi2018stabilized} predict for two such electrons sharing a resonator mode, and
is this the more promising route for distant-electron entanglement (e.g.\ across a sensor network,
cf.\ \texttt{quantum-sensing}'s proposed network extension) compared to the direct-Coulomb route at
the separations where both are in principle available? This remains the least-constrained of the
three protocols and the natural next target now that the single-electron strong-coupling prerequisite
is no longer in question.

\section{Results}

Not yet available. Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong} establishes the cooperativity/sideband-resolution
budget this section needs as an \emph{input} ($g/2\pi=118\pm3\,\mathrm{MHz}$,
$\kappa/2\pi=23\,\mathrm{MHz}$, $\Gamma_2/2\pi=61$--$75\,\mathrm{MHz}$,
$\mathcal{C}\approx32$, resolved sideband $\kappa/\omega_e\sim3\times10^{-3}$), but this section will
still report, for each of the three protocols, the achievable figure of merit computed from those
numbers: final phonon occupation for cooling (Sec.~\ref{sec:cooling}), squeezing in dB for squeezing
(Sec.~\ref{sec:squeezing}), and logarithmic negativity or an entanglement witness for the two-electron
case (Sec.~\ref{sec:entanglement}, which additionally requires extending the single-electron result to
a two-electron, shared-resonator design that does not yet exist).

\section{Discussion and outlook}

With the strong-coupling and resolved-sideband prerequisites now experimentally established by
Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong}, the immediate task for this project shifts from "is a cooperativity
budget favorable at all" to computing the three protocols' actual figures of merit from the measured
$g$, $\kappa$, and (temperature-dependent, dephasing-dominated) $\Gamma_2(T)$, and checking whether
the observed non-saturating low-temperature dephasing (Sec.~\ref{sec:cooling}) caps the achievable
cooling/squeezing performance below what $\mathcal{C}\approx32$ alone would suggest. The entanglement
protocol (Sec.~\ref{sec:entanglement}) is the one channel Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong} does not
directly address — it demonstrates single-electron, single-dot strong coupling only — so a two-electron
shared- or coupled-resonator extension of that device is the concrete design question to raise with
the experimental collaborators next. If the dissipative entanglement route proves competitive with the
existing Coulomb-coupling route, it is worth quantifying head-to-head using the same fidelity/entropy
metrics as \texttt{coulomb-entanglement}, reusing the numerical toolkit in
\texttt{ci-dvr-hartree-numerics} extended to include cavity dissipation (a Lindblad treatment, currently
flagged as future work for this project's decoherence modeling more broadly). Separately,
Ref.~\cite{koolstra2026strong}'s own outlook toward spin readout via spin--orbit hybridization (a
micromagnet-induced field gradient, following semiconductor-quantum-dot precedent) is a complementary,
not competing, use of the same strong-coupling hardware and is better tracked under
\texttt{quantum-sensing}/\texttt{charge-readout} than folded into this project.
